Watching a model think: the depth budget

results in

Ask a model to add four numbers “in its head” — no scratch work allowed. Inside, there are no loops: one pass through 32 processing layers, like an assembly line. So where do the one-after-another steps happen? A new interpretability tool (open-sourced by Anthropic this year) lets us watch answers form layer by layer.

  • Each added step pushes the answer about one layer deeper into the network — and when a problem needs more layers than the model has, it starts getting the answer wrong, right about where the arithmetic predicts.
  • The model also caches partial results when it can see the task coming. Take the cache away and accuracy drops, but the layer-by-layer pattern stays — the deep computation is real, not an artifact of the prompt.
  • The surprise: the tool’s readout can be edited without changing the model’s answer (82 of 95 edits “took” in the readout; the answer followed in only 9). What a model’s internals show is not automatically what’s doing the work — a caution for every interpretability claim, including ours. A 70-billion-parameter model also broke the tidy story in informative ways — details below.

Why it matters: the agenda needs trustworthy readouts of what models are doing inside. This is what building — and stress-testing — that ground truth looks like, for tens of dollars of compute.

Full detail (methods, numbers, caveats)

Question. A transformer answers 13 + 24 + 8 + 21 silently — no scratch work, just the number — in a single fixed-length pass through its layers. But the architecture has no loops, and adding four numbers means doing the steps one after another. So where do the serial steps physically go inside the network? This matters to the broader agenda because reading a model's honest internal state presupposes understanding the substrate that carries its intermediate "thoughts" — you can't trust a readout of a computation you can't locate.

      • Serial steps cost depth, at about one layer per step. The layer where the final answer first becomes readable climbs steadily with chain length — layer 18 (of 32) for one addition, layer 25 for eight — about 0.8–1.0 layers per added step. A control that looks like a long sum but requires no work (10 + 10 + 10 + …) stays flat around layers 19–22, so it is the computation that costs depth, not the length of the prompt.
      • That depth budget predicts where the model fails. Accuracy is perfect at three additions (1.00), falls to 0.79 at six, 0.25 at eight, and 0.00 at twelve. Extending the one-layer-per-step line puts the twelve-addition answer at about layer 30 — the model's final "output" layers — which is exactly where accuracy hits zero. The errors are near-misses (a dropped partial sum), not nonsense.
      • A cache, and what happens when you remove it. When the task is announced up front (calc: 13 + 24 + …), the model computes partial sums early and parks them along earlier words — 21% of intermediate sums are readable there. Hide the task until the last tokens (13 24 8 21, sum:) and that cache drops to 9%, the computation relocates to the final position (intermediates found there rise from 16% to 27%), and accuracy at six additions falls from 79% to 29%. The cache was doing real work — yet the answer's emergence depth is identical with or without it, so the cache buys behavior, not depth.
      • A sharp causal boundary at layer 15. Inject "the running total is 3 bigger" directly into the final position's internal state, one layer at a time. Below layer 15: nothing, even though the injected signal is physically present. From layer 15 on: the answer moves by exactly the injected amount, every time, regardless of which operand you perturb or how long the problem is. So the operands all arrive in parallel at a fixed coupling depth; the step-by-step cost lives in the combination that happens after.
      • Watchable serial computation, with nowhere to hide. In the alphabet task ("5 letters after L:"), the intermediate letters (M, N, O, P) appear nowhere in the prompt and can't be cached. On problems the model gets right, they materialize in sequence at deep layers of the final position — hop one readable around layer 23, the answer around layer 25.
      • Reportable is not the same as load-bearing. A hardening experiment swapped the lens-visible letter at the frontier. The swap "took" in the readout (82 of 95 cases moved the lens-visible letter to the injected one) but the model's actual output followed in only 9 of those 82. The thing the lens shows you at the frontier is editable without the computation caring — a genuine dissociation between what is readable and what is doing the work. (This sits in some tension with swap results in the source paper on a larger model, and is stated cautiously for that reason.)
        • The strongest version of the claim — that the same circuit is literally re-applied at successive depths — is not shown. The causal test for it (clamp the ladder's current letter and check the remaining hops run from the clamped value) returned a null on the 8B, and a follow-up on the 70B was instrument-limited: additive lens-direction swaps don't achieve clean semantic replacement. After two limited attempts the re-application question is still open; the planned round-two tool is a projection-based replacement rather than an additive injection.
        • One architecture (Llama), essentially one task family. A Qwen replication failed at the starting line — Qwen3-8B can't do the alphabet task at all — so cross-family evidence is currently zero.
        • The lens is fitted on ordinary web text and applied to arithmetic; a plain logit-lens control agrees on direction, but absolute layer numbers carry uncertainty.
        • The cache-causality ablation used single-direction projection, the weakest form of ablation, so its "cache is epiphenomenal at first order" reading is a soft one.
        • Several intermediate results were wrong and were kept visible on purpose: a tokenizer artifact that hid all answer emergence, an exciting "the model secretly knew the right answer" signal that dissolved under a proper control, and a first synthesis that made a clean prediction and failed within the hour.
