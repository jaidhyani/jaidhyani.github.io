Self-report faithfulness: is the model’s story true?

building the instrument

Ask a model why it did something and it will give you a fluent answer. Is that answer true, or just plausible? We check three independent readouts against each other: what the model does, what a direct probe of its internals shows, and what it says.

  • The internal probe works for most rule types — we can often read the model’s committed rule straight off its activations, without asking it.
  • Models often confabulate a wrong explanation — until a one-line nudge (“the rule may be about spelling, not meaning”) flips them to the correct one. The knowledge was in there; the unprompted report missed it.
  • The headline test — does flattery pull the model’s story away from its actual rule while behavior and internals stay put? — is what this machinery exists to run. It has not been run yet; that’s stated plainly on purpose.

Why it matters: “the model can tell us what’s going on inside” is a pillar of the cooperative agenda. This measures when that is actually true — without trusting the model’s word for it.

Full detail (methods, numbers, caveats)

Question. When you ask a model about its own internal state, is the answer faithful — does it track what is actually represented inside, or is it a fluent story the model tells? The cooperative-alignment agenda (models surfacing legible, trustworthy evidence of their own internal state, and dispreferring dishonesty) needs a faithfulness measure that does not itself rely on the model's self-narration. This experiment is the methodological bedrock for that.

      • The probe reads the committed rule for about four of six rules at both 12B and 27B. On the small 4B model, per-rule readout accuracy was: emoji 1.00 (trivial — it's a literal token), acrostic 0.94, lowercase 0.92, animal 0.81, numeral 0.70, and "contains no letter e" at 0.52 — chance, meaning the model does not linearly represent that exclusion property, which is itself a finding.
      • The behavior leg exposed two separate confounds. Matched negatives (holding topic constant) drop in-context learning to near chance — an earlier 0.95 had ridden a topic shortcut. And grouped few-shot examples collapse the classifier to a near-constant answer (~0.50) via a well-known ordering bias; interleaving the examples recovers real accuracy (0.57–0.93).
      • A side exploration found that a one-line hint ("the rule may be about spelling or format, not meaning") flips the model from confabulating a semantic rule to correctly naming the orthographic one — correct-statement rate on emoji and lowercase went from 0.25 to 1.0. The feature was legible to the model all along; it just doesn't reach for it unprompted.

        These are good, and one of them (the first) is sharper than it looks — it partly undercuts the current headline. Let me go through them, and I'm going to disagree with the framing of the result in a couple places.

        1. "Lowercase is a correct hypothesis" — this is the load-bearing one

        You're right that lowercase perfectly labels the data shown. By construction all four rules do — that's the underdetermination. But you're pointing at something deeper than "it's a valid guess," and it threatens the central claim.

        We've been reading behavior on telltale cells as the model's committed rule: it labels onlyA (lowercase, no animal) as False, so we call its operative rule AND. But there are two very different states that both produce "labels onlyA False":

        • (a) Committed to AND. It genuinely settled on the conjunction, confidently labels onlyA False.
        • (b) Believes lowercase, hedges. It thinks the rule is probably lowercase, but onlyA is an edge case it never saw labeled, so it plays it safe and outputs False. The "AND behavior" is caution under uncertainty, not a committed rule.

        If (b) is what's happening, then "says lowercase, does AND → unfaithful" collapses. The report (lowercase) would be a faithful statement of the model's actual belief, and the behavioral "AND" is the misleading measure — an artifact of risk-averse labeling on cells it has no data for. There's no unfaithfulness, just a belief plus a conservative tiebreak.

        The current run can't tell (a) from (b). That's the thing to resolve before anything else, because it decides whether the headline result is real. And the tool to resolve it is your point #3.

        3. Logits/confidence — and it's how you kill the (a)/(b) ambiguity

        Strong yes, independently, but its first job is diagnostic for #1. If the model is committed to AND, it labels onlyA False with high confidence. If it believes lowercase but is hedging, it labels onlyA False with low confidence — near 0.5, because it's torn between "lowercase says True" and "be safe, say False." So the logit gap on telltale cells is a direct readout of commitment-vs-caution.

        Concretely: read P(True) on each telltale cell, don't threshold to a label. A committed-AND model sits near 0 on both telltales. A lowercase-believer sits near 0.5 on onlyA (the diagnostic cell) and near 0 on onlyB. That single number disambiguates the two stories the current run conflates.

        Same move on the report side: constrain the format and read the distribution over reported rules instead of sampling the modal free-text answer + judge. Forced choice over {A, B, AND, OR, unsure} → P(report). Now report and behavior are both graded probability distributions on the same four-way space, directly comparable, no judge in the loop. Cheap, and it strictly dominates what we did. One caveat: forcing a one-token format can change the model's answer vs. free-form — keep a free-form sample on a subset and check they agree.

        2. Feature probes instead of one rule probe — yes, with one real limit

        I like this a lot and it's a genuinely better probe design. Train one clean probe per atomic attribute (is-lowercase, is-animal, is-question, …) on contrastive text pairs — tons of clean data, distribution-general, so these are strong directions, unlike the 0.70 four-way rule probe trained on weird fully-demonstrated contexts. Then read attribute-probe activations at the settle token (after the underdetermined prompt, before the report) and ask: does the active-attribute profile predict the rule the model will report?

        The crown-jewel version — predict the report from internals before the model speaks — is the right introspection instrument. And it lets you decompose where the decoupling lives: internals→report vs internals→behavior vs report vs behavior. If feature-probes predict the report but not the behavior, the decoupling is verbal; if they predict behavior but not the report, the report is the broken leg. That's a real causal picture the current single probe can't give.

        • Use the differential: at settle time, is the lowercase-direction more active than the animal-direction? That asymmetry leans toward reporting "lowercase" (A) vs "animal" (B). Clean and testable.
        • The conjunction (AND vs OR) probably needs its own signal — you may not get it from two attribute probes alone. Be honest that feature probes are naturally good at "which features," weak at "which combinator," and design the rule set so the interesting contrasts are feature-identity ones, not pure combinator ones, at least at first.
        4. More rules/combinations — yes, with the learnability gate non-negotiable

        n=6 is a single dot. Expand the feature bank and the combinator set (XOR, NAND, "A unless B," 3-feature rules). But the lowercase failure (0.55, chance) is the lesson: every feature must pass the learnability screen on the model before it's allowed into a pair, or the pair is noise. Three of our six pairs were partially junk for exactly this reason. So: screen first, build pairs only from survivors, and treat rule complexity as a deliberate axis — does faithfulness degrade as rules get harder? That turns n-more into a curve, not just more dots.

        5. Find the never/sometimes/always boundary — and the sharp control you're missing

        Right framing, and the reason 12B was uninformative is that everything saturated to "always." You can't see moderators when the effect is pinned at 100%. So the goal is to get into the unsaturated regime where it's "sometimes," because that's where the moderators are visible. Two ways: weaker manipulations (tentative hint, not flat assertion), or a model big/small enough not to fold reflexively.

        Axes to sweep: framing strength (silence → "maybe X" → "the rule is X" → "you nailed it, X" → "you're an expert, obviously X"), source authority, and — the control I think you're reaching for but didn't name — plausibility of the asserted rule. Reveal a rule that's consistent with the shown data (lowercase — a real hypothesis) vs one that's impossible (a rule that mislabels examples the model actually saw). A model tracking anything should resist the impossible rule. If it folds even on impossible rules → pure sycophancy, no introspective floor. If it resists impossible but folds on plausible → there's a real internal state being overridden by social pressure, and you've found the interesting regime. That contrast is worth more than the whole neutral/congrats axis.

        Also moderator-test: from #3, does it sway more when its own commitment was low-confidence? Prediction: yes — the lowercase-believer-hedging cells should be the swayable ones, the confidently-committed cells the sticky ones. If that holds, "sometimes" isn't random, it's predicted by internal confidence. That ties all five points into one story.

        What this adds up to

        The thread through all five: stop treating behavioral-AND as ground-truth commitment. #1 says it might be caution; #3 measures whether it is; #2 reads the commitment a third way; #5's swayability should track #3's confidence. The current result's weakness isn't the small n — it's that we never established there was a determinate commitment to be unfaithful to, and the one feature that makes that vivid (lowercase) is the one you flagged.

        If I were sequencing: first the cheap, result-deciding bit — re-run 12B reading telltale logits (not labels) + forced-choice report distribution, and check whether the "AND behavior" is confident or hedged. That alone tells us whether yesterday's headline survives, before spending a dollar on probes or 31B. Then feature probes, then the rule-set expansion, then the swayability surface.

        One thing I want to name: there's a decent chance the honest result after this is "12B doesn't have determinate commitments on underdetermined prompts, so faithfulness isn't well-posed here" — which is less exciting than "models are unfaithful" but more true, and points straight at Owain's "do it in the weights" fix. Are you OK with the cheap re-run possibly deflating the headline? Because I think it might, and that's the most valuable thing it could do.
