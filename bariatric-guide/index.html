Chapter 7 · Vitamins & Minerals Why You Need Supplements
Section 01

Why every patient
needs supplements.

After bariatric surgery, you will not be able to get enough vitamins and minerals from food alone. This is not a matter of will or diet quality — it is a permanent change to how your body absorbs nutrients. Supplements are for life.

What changes after surgery

All bariatric procedures reduce how much food you eat. Procedures that bypass parts of the small intestine — Roux-en-Y, SADI, and Duodenal Switch — also reduce how much your body absorbs from that food. The result is a real risk of deficiency, even in patients who eat well.

The deficiencies we watch for

Thiamine (B1) Can cause permanent neurological injury. Highest risk in the first 6 months and during any prolonged vomiting.
Iron, Folate, B12 Cause anemia — fatigue, weakness, cold intolerance, brittle nails.
Calcium & Vitamin D Bone loss, osteoporosis, fractures. Damage accumulates silently for years.
Zinc & Copper Hair loss, skin and nail changes, altered taste, weakened immunity.
Vitamins A, E, K Higher risk after SADI and DS — fat-soluble vitamins depend on the section of intestine that is bypassed.
Folate — pregnancy Folate deficiency can cause neural-tube birth defects. Critical for any patient of childbearing potential.
● How this guide works

Section 02 lists the daily nutrient targets your body actually needs. Sections 03 and 04 give you two ways to meet those targets — a single-product bariatric regimen and a budget-friendly mix-and-match. The final page is a tear-out reference for your fridge.

! Form matters

Calcium must be citrate, not carbonate — you no longer make enough stomach acid to absorb the carbonate form. No gummy multivitamins — they lack minerals and some B vitamins. Do not crush enteric-coated pills.

Chapter 7 · Section 03 · Group A Sleeve / Roux-en-Y Regimen
Section 03 · Group A

If you had a Sleeve
or Roux-en-Y Bypass.

Pick the option that fits your budget. Both options meet the daily targets in Section 02. The simpler option uses one bariatric-specific multivitamin; the budget option mixes drugstore products to reach the same totals.

Option 1 · Simpler

Bariatric MVI + Calcium Citrate

~ $35–$50 per month
What to buy
  • 1 bariatric chewable MVI with iron, twice daily (or one dose if labeled "complete" for bariatric use)
  • Calcium citrate + D3, 500–600 mg, twice daily
Recommended brands

Bariatric Advantage Ultra Solo with Iron · Celebrate Multi with Iron · ProCare Health Bariatric · BariMelts Complete

Option 2 · Budget

Drugstore mix & match

~ $15–$25 per month
What to buy
  • Centrum or Flintstones Complete chewable, two per day
  • Calcium citrate + D3, 500–600 mg, twice daily (Citracal Maximum)
  • Sublingual B12, 1,000 µg daily
  • Iron 18 mg if you menstruate (or 45–60 mg ferrous sulfate)
Why two MVIs

A single drugstore MVI does not meet bariatric thiamine and folate targets. Two doses, spaced morning and evening, do.

Chapter 7 · Section 04 · Group B SADI / Duodenal Switch Regimen
Section 04 · Group B

If you had a SADI
or Duodenal Switch.

SADI and Duodenal Switch bypass more of the small intestine — including the section that absorbs fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K). Your regimen is larger than Group A's, and a standard drugstore MVI is not enough on its own. Lifelong lab work every 6–12 months is non-negotiable.

! Why this section is different

After SADI/DS you malabsorb fat. Vitamins A, D, E, K are fat-soluble — without supplementation, deficiencies are not a risk, they are expected. Night blindness, bone disease, easy bruising, and clotting problems all trace back to skipping this regimen.

Option 1 · Simpler

DS-Specific Bariatric MVI + Calcium

~ $55–$90 per month
What to buy
  • DS- or BPD-formulated chewable MVI, twice daily — contains higher iron, zinc, copper, and water-miscible A/D/E/K
  • Calcium citrate + D3, 600 mg, three times daily (1,800 mg total)
  • Sublingual B12, 1,000 µg daily
Recommended brands

Celebrate Bariatric Three Multi · Bariatric Advantage Advanced EA Multi · ProCare Health Bariatric (DS-formulated) · Bariatric Fusion BPD/DS

Option 2 · Budget

Drugstore mix & match

~ $30–$45 per month
What to buy
  • Centrum or Flintstones Complete chewable, two per day
  • Calcium citrate + D3, 600 mg × 3 daily (Citracal Maximum)
  • Vitamin D3, additional 2,000 IU daily
  • Vitamin A (water-miscible), 10,000 IU daily
  • Vitamin K1, 300 µg daily
  • Zinc 22 mg + Copper 2 mg daily (or zinc-balanced complex)
  • Iron 45–60 mg ferrous sulfate, daily
  • Sublingual B12, 1,000 µg daily
Notes

The à-la-carte route works but takes more daily attention. For most DS patients we recommend Option 1 once it fits the budget — adherence is the single biggest predictor of long-term outcomes.

Lab schedule for DS patients

DS patients have the highest deficiency risk of any bariatric procedure. Lab work is not optional — it is how we catch problems before they become symptoms.

! Required labs

Frequency: Every 3 months for the first year, then every 6 months for life.

Panel: CBC, iron panel, ferritin, B12, folate, 25-OH vitamin D, calcium + PTH, magnesium, zinc, copper, vitamin A, vitamin E, INR (as proxy for vitamin K), thiamine.

Bring this guide and your supplement bottles to every appointment. If a brand changes or you switch from one option to another, tell us — the doses are not always interchangeable.

Chapter 7 · Section 05 Absorption Rules
Section 05

The rules that make
your supplements work.

Vitamins and minerals interact. Two pills you took correctly can still leave you deficient if you took them at the wrong time, with the wrong drink, or against the wrong food. These rules are short — memorize them.

Timing

  • Calcium and iron compete — never take them within 2 hours of each other.
  • Calcium and thyroid compete — separate by 2 hr. Iron and thyroid: 4 hr.
  • Wait 1 hour between any supplement and any prescription medication.
  • Never more than 500–600 mg of calcium at one time — your body can't absorb more.

What to take it with

  • Plain water only. Coffee, tea, and milk interfere with mineral absorption.
  • Iron with vitamin C — small glass of OJ or a 250 mg tablet. Big absorption boost.
  • Iron on an empty stomach if you tolerate it; otherwise a small protein snack.
  • All other supplements with food — better absorption, less nausea.

Forms to avoid

  • No gummy MVIs — they lack minerals and B vitamins.
  • No calcium carbonate — citrate only. You no longer have the acid to absorb it.
  • No crushing of time-release or enteric-coated tablets.
  • No pills larger than your fingertip for the first month after surgery.

Habits to keep

  • Take everything every day. A missed week of B12 is fine; a missed month of thiamine is not.
  • Bloodwork on schedule — every 3 months year 1, then 6–12 months for life.
  • Tell every provider you had bariatric surgery — especially before any new prescription.
  • If you can't take it or afford it — call us. Don't just stop.
! Call your surgeon if

Persistent vomiting, confusion, memory problems, numbness or tingling, vision changes, easy bruising, persistent diarrhea, or unexplained weight loss. These can be early signs of thiamine, B12, or fat-soluble vitamin deficiency.
