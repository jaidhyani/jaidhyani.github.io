LLMs are not Next-Token Predictors

an interactive rant

LLMs are not next-token predictors.

I don't mean they're not just next-token predictors. I'm not referring to capabilities that emerge from post-training. I'm not claiming any unique insight or breakthrough.

I'm saying that good old-fashioned transformer-decoder LLMs pretrained on large corpuses are optimized for something other than next-token prediction. That this is a straightforward and mathematically demonstrable fact. That it's a subtle but important difference. And that the slightly inaccurate way we typically talk about LLMs confuses our thinking and intuitions around them.

I'm not really making an argument. I'm describing what's already there in plain sight, and how it doesn't really line up with the story we've told so many times that even the LLMs themselves have internalized it.

(Spoiler for the punchline: kv activations are important and backprop flows from each token prediction to every previous forward pass through them.)

There are at least two important falsehoods implicit in this understanding of LLMs:

  1. LLMs have no persistent internal state aside from their fixed weights — there is the token stream, and nothing more.

  2. Each forward pass receives training signal only from its own prediction. We never reach back and adjust earlier-position computations to improve a later answer.

If both were true, the LLM would in fact be just a next-token predictor. Neither is.

Here's a more accurate (but still simplified) model of an LLM in action, incorporating KV activations.

Every attention head in a transformer really has two outputs: there's an additive write to the residual stream, and there are the KV activations. The KV activations are persistent internal state for the LLM, and will inform every subsequent layer in future forward passes. Information flows down through layers within a forward pass, but sideways1 across different forward passes within attention mechanisms on each layer.

Anyone who spends any time studying LLMs understands that KV activations store persistent state, but it's completely lost in the "LLMs are just next-token-predictors" story. And as I'll argue, telling that story often enough can cause even people who understand KV activations as persistent state to discount that aspect.

Now consider backpropagation. We compare each forward pass's output to the ground-truth next token and compute the gradient of every parameter with respect to the resulting loss. The loss is next-token cross-entropy, summed over every position in the document.

Because every KV activation informs downstream layer activations on every subsequent forward pass, gradient from position N's prediction flows back through the KV state at every earlier position. Every KV-relevant weight gets gradient from every future token's prediction. And every parameter in the model is upstream of some KV.

And now we can see that the second claim — that backprop only affects weights with regard to next-token prediction — is also false. On the contrary: every parameter is optimized to predict every future token that will appear in the context.

LLMs are not next-token predictors. They are full-context predictors whose visible output happens to be one token at a time.

In substituting mere-next-token-prediction as the main intuition for thinking about LLMs, a lot of discussion ends up obscured or confused on important points.

1Most LLM state lives in KV activations.

We're usually very aware of this when considering hardware requirements and cache economics. It tends to fall by the wayside elsewhere. For example, it was — and for some providers still is — common to redact the reasoning block of a reasoning model's response from future turns. The model generates <reasoning><response>; on the next turn we remove <reasoning> from history and the model sees only that it said <response>. This saves context tokens, at the cost of obliterating not just the KV activations of the reasoning, but also the KV activations of the response itself — which must now be recomputed as if <response> had been produced without any reasoning at all.

It would be as if a human suddenly forgot the thought process that led to something they said, and had to instantaneously construct a fabricated history of what they were thinking when they said it — which may or may not diverge wildly from what they were actually thinking. It's perhaps for this reason that Anthropic switched to preserving thinking blocks by default as of Opus 4.5. The same dynamic plausibly underlies anecdotal reports of coding assistants becoming much less effective after compaction events, even when the compacted context retains all the semantically important content: too much of the state was in the KV cache, completely inaccessible to the compaction process, and irreversibly lost afterward.

2LLMs (and/or their personas) may have inaccurate self-models.

"Small Models Can Introspect Too" built on Anthropic's "Emergent Introspective Awareness in Large Language Models" to show that not only could surprisingly small models detect concept injections, but that this capability improved when the context included (a) an explanation of how KV activations could mechanically enable some degree of introspection, and (b) the abstract of the Anthropic paper. This suggests that introspection capabilities in the absence of that context were effectively sandbagged — the LLM acted as if it lacked a capability that it had — and that some portion of the effect plausibly derives from inaccurate self-modeling.

If there are measurable effects from making the persona aware of KV mechanics, there could be more impact from the realization that LLMs are always engaged in long-term prediction at a fundamental architectural level. This has implications for evaluation and oversight. Beyond the sandbagging concern, models with inaccurate self-models may be more likely to confabulate explanations for their own behavior, or to make less effective use of evidence available via introspection. And monitoring efforts that rely on legible token-output traces may face an "awareness overhang" as models become more aware of latent long-term-planning capabilities that bypass the token stream entirely.

3The "next-token predictor" framing distorts the broader discourse.

The volume of discussion built on top of the "just a next-token predictor" assumption tends to promote and exacerbate misconceptions. It seems likely, for example, that this encourages people to be more dismissive of current and potential LLM capabilities. Questions of latent long-term planning simply don't come up — but on architectural grounds alone, they should be a higher priority.

4Intuitions about model experience and welfare should reflect this.

Whatever you think LLMs are doing, your model of their experience and moral standing should account for the fact that most of their state — and most of what they're being optimized for — lives outside the narrow channel through which they talk to us.
