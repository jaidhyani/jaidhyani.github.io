About this page

Maa Ji is a privately published tribute booklet (given free, "best wishes" in place of a price) collecting remembrances of Smt. Durga Devi Khanduri — known in childhood as Anandi — by her son Mohan Khanduri and other family members. It records her life from a wealthy Garhwali household into a path of khadi, freedom-struggle activism, women's organizing, and lifelong service in the hills around Pauri.

Mother–Father (poem)

Life runs on breath, on the countless gifts of nature; everywhere life is nourished, from God's priceless treasury. How much we receive — and what all we receive — who really knows? It goes on, ceaseless, life keeps receiving life. In this play of giving life to life, in this wondrous game of nature, God has shaped a few beings unlike any other — who are dearest of all, rarest of all. They are simply the ones who give life to life. Unaware even of their own greatness, they are purely givers; they are known by the name "mother and father," they are the ones who give us life. They hand the whole of themselves to us, shielding us with their own bodies, they save us from sorrow, pain, scorching sun and cold. They pour their whole selves out as an offering over us, fill our laps with joys, and without wanting, without asking, one day they fly far away — they are the ones we know by the name "mother and father."

Our Mother and Father (poem)

I carry this thread a little further, and tell a little about my own mother and father. They were different, utterly singular, not known like ordinary parents. Laughing, weeping, singing, spending themselves through joy and sorrow, growing tired, at ease, ill at ease, in the name of every truth, with every firm step of their own, they were not just the ones who gave us life — they were the ones who made us good human beings. Compassion, righteousness, forgiveness, the laws of knowledge and science, restraint, worship, charity — they taught us all of this. How much of it we managed to live is another story, but the mother and father who were ours were going to make us into something set apart from the world. It is only by their grace that we go on, by such grace of God that we are nourished. We bow our heads to mother and father; we offer them our reverence.

Dedication

There are many things whose beauty survives precisely because they are not spoken of — and that beauty might not survive if they were. The word "Mother" is one such: in all the world it is supreme, the most universal, all-encompassing, honored, pure, unshakable and good. And then, if the mother is Durga herself — head bowed to you, O mother, giver of birth: the guidance you gave was joy-giving, God-given, hard but excellent.

Missing an Opportunity (poem)

The news doesn't reach us — dazzle surrounds us, layer upon layer of ignorance. Taking mother and father to be merely a relationship, we miss a great opportunity, failing to recognize its dignity. We were very fortunate — we who, like victory-Durga, found a father and mother to show us life's path. Wherever we stand today, we say with pride: it was mother and father who gave us this opportunity.

The Struggle of Leaving Dehradun

The late Shri Bahuguna — Mamaji (maternal uncle) — would sometimes recount old matters, but this episode he told me one day in Lucknow at Darul Shafa, when I had come from Jaipur (1964–65) to stay with him and was looking for work. Amid his extreme busyness he got a chance to come home for lunch, and as we ate he said — "Maldar Sahab (he called me 'Maldar Sahab' or 'Doctor Sahab'), let me tell you a 1942 story about my elder sister."

In her stubbornness, Didi left Dehradun. Whatever goods she could carry she took and left at our elder sister's (Deepa Mausi's) house in Pauri, where there was little room. As luck had it I was in Pauri then, and she asked me to arrange a horse or two to carry some belongings to her in-laws' village, Budhani. The horses were arranged; we loaded goods, boxes and children and set off. Just as we neared the village a heavy box slipped off a horse and fell into a gully below. With so much luggage, the children, and dusk falling, she said, "Go with the horseman and bring that box up." I was furious, but in front of Didi's situation that anger was useless. So I climbed down into the dark hilly river valley, searched, and fortunately the box hadn't opened — but how we hauled that heavy box back up I still haven't forgotten. Doctor, your mother's story of struggle is a very great one. She willingly gave up such prosperity and chose the path of struggle on the strength of nothing but the courage of her convictions — and she succeeded.

Earliest Memories and Those Values

Even today I remember those days in the upper bazaar of Pauri — that two-story house with eight small and large rooms.

I slept beside Mother. She would hum me to sleep in her sweet voice with songs of Ram and Krishna; but before sleeping she always had us bow to Hanuman ji. And at dawn, in the Brahma-muhurta, amid the chirping of birds and the ringing of the worship bell and conch from Father's room, Mother would lift and wake us. That majestic hour — the atmosphere, the purity — settled so deeply into our lives that it stayed with us forever. Whether we managed to absorb the calm, the serenity, the steadfastness, humanity, compassion and tolerance born of it is another matter; but it is true that Mother's song-filled lullabies were a great support in keeping us from going astray.

It is by Mother's grace alone that we are here. In life's most precious value — the way to live as a human being — our Mother gave us full guidance. This is our great fortune and the grace of God, the gods, and our ancestors.

Our Mother believed in living properly and freely — and to make that propriety pure, the path was not force or harshness but making one's own conduct full of humanity, befriending the soul, until that freedom became self-realized and one's conduct flawless and humane. In such an environment Smt. Durga Devi Khanduri raised us, and paved the way for us to move forward without any ego and without any inferiority complex.

My Mother's Definition of God's Grace

This was around 1955–56. I was in Class 7 at Messmore Inter College, Pauri. Mother's health worsened and would not improve even after a month and a half; things grew worse by the day. All night we children would pass the time in such uncertainty.

In the mornings, though, Mother would gesture for me to go to school even when I had no wish to — and, truthfully, I had begun to fear for her life. I went daily, praying only that she stay well; all day the worry stayed with me. When I returned around one-thirty I would pray that there be no crowd gathered before our house and that Mother be well (you understand what I mean).

Child though I was, one day I could not bear her suffering and asked her — "Mother, all over Pauri people speak of your goodness, your charity, your help to the poor and helpless — so why must you bear so much pain?" Her answer has left its imprint on my mind to this day. Mother said — "Son, despite doing so much good I must suffer this much; so think — if I had not done all this good, what a terrible state I'd be in. So keep doing good deeds; God is just and impartial." By God's grace she became completely well, but she gave me, for life, the true meaning of human deeds and God's grace. Such was our Mother, Durga Devi Khanduri.

Bahuguna Mamaji and Mother

Our Mamaji and our Mother — though separate in personality, how many similarities there were; it is astonishing. I count it my good fortune to have been, for a long stretch, very close to Mamaji, and to have known him closely; and Mother, of course, is Mother. Both were great souls, extraordinary even in an ordinary setting. About Mamaji, years ago (after his passing) I drew out some reminiscences, like a tribute of flowers, in the English newspaper I had published from Lucknow, Times and Reflection. About Mother, too, I once brought out a special issue of my Hindi magazine Abhivyakti ki Or with memories of her; she read it all, kept copies, and I felt she had liked it — which gave me great satisfaction.

They were partners in countless episodes, but here is one. Around 1974–75 in Lucknow, Mamaji was Chief Minister of U.P., and a senior Congress convention was held at Begum Hazrat Mahal Park, with a government development exhibition where our organization had a pavilion. Prime Minister Smt. Indira Gandhi was to address it. Our Mother, Durga Devi Khanduri, was there with the Garhwal Congress contingent…

…Bahuguna ji's words at the rally

Before Indira ji's address, Chief Minister Shri Bahuguna told the convention: "I am delighted that thousands upon thousands have come from all over the state to welcome Indira ji. And, Indira ji, it gives me joy and pride to tell you that somewhere in this crowd my own sister is sitting with the hill contingent — my sister Durga Devi Khanduri, who, when I lived with her in Dehradun, inspired me to take part in the freedom struggle. Even then, the daughter-in-law of a wealthy family, she had begun wearing khadi; and it was she who bought me my first bicycle, at Darshanlal Chowraha, Dehradun." Since I was present at the exhibition I got to hear this. Such was the goodwill and love between our Mother and Bahuguna ji.

Emotional Support in Bahuguna ji's Struggle

This is from the days when Mamaji had come out of jail after imprisonment in the freedom struggle in Allahabad and lived a life of struggle — his whole life dedicated to the country and Congress, with no settled arrangement for daily bread. Kamla Mamiji too was a freedom fighter, with the responsibility of raising the children. Mamaji lived at 12 Hastings Road, Allahabad; for a living he had 5–6 rickshaws plied, and was a leader of the labour movement and factory workers; yet financial hardship persisted.

In those days he kept in constant touch with Mother by letter, and she sensed her brother's difficulties. When I was small, Mother would take her small children to his house in Allahabad, learn the situation, but could not give him money — her own large family was in difficulty. Later I was told that since Mother had enough gold, she would give some gold to Mamaji. He remembered this all his life, and the two always exchanged views and help on family, personal, social and political matters. Mamaji too always gave our family abundant help.

Maa Ji's Ancestral Village, and Reverence for the Ancestors

Maa Ji was married perhaps around age 13. She came from a well-to-do family, her childhood spent in a progressive, religious and cultured background, and after marriage she was wed to a boy from a big, wealthy, English-educated Dehradun family — though that family too lived in an atmosphere of Vedic culture, wintering in Dehradun, summering in Mussoorie, and spending 2–3 months at their village Margadana near Pauri. They say the village house had 50 rooms; when I saw it later there weren't 50, but from the many broken-down rooms it seemed it must have been true. The temple of our family goddess, Maa Dyurari Devi, stood inside this house, and by Mother's grace we worshipped there many times. The wealthy household's whole caravan — servants, horses, carriages — would come and reside, with farming, forest, gardens, wells: the air of a princely estate. But Mother told us she too worked alongside the household women — tending cows, cutting grass and wood, doing farm work as an equal; there was no discrimination. Later Grandfather gave these workers land and houses, settling them in villages like Mawar, Udalyun and Dhanak, whose descendants own that land today.

…the village (continued)

When Mother and Father left Dehradun in 1942 and came to Pauri, the care of the village became theirs too; "Khekar" (to whom we had given land on shares) gave us wheat and rice. We usually came on foot from Pauri for the worship of our family goddess, even though the Pauri–Badrinath road had been cut right between our house and our garden. We had a special affection for this village. As circumstances changed and education and health improved, Maa Ji gave her own land and built a primary school, had it made a middle school, and later built another middle school nearby. These schools still run at the government level, with children from surrounding villages studying there.

Radhagram

Maa Ji often said there was no memorial in the name of the youngest father-in-law, the late Radhavallabh ji ("Maldar"). (In other elders' names there were already the Chandra Ballabh Trust and Ghananand Inter College, Mussoorie.) So something ought to be made in his name, and finally, through years of tireless effort, she got the government to name our village Margadana "Radhagram." Today on the Pauri–Srinagar road a signboard reading "Radhagram" stands below our village house, and we feel proud to see it. Such extraordinary works, done by a household woman loaded with family burdens, are rarely seen. We count ourselves blessed to have had such a Mother.

Bahuguna ji and Emotional Support (continued)

Bahuguna Mamaji's life remained a struggle despite his industry, valour and political acumen; and so, leaving aside politics, was our Mother's. Both lived their whole lives struggling, apart from self-interest, yet both left an indelible mark in their fields. His name is still spoken across the country and abroad; and our Mother's name too is still taken, for her many-sided personality — perhaps why the two stayed lifelong close through each other's joys and sorrows.

Mahila Mandal (Women's Association)

Sometimes I think — a woman struggling through life with 8 children and a husband, how could she think beyond that family? But our Mother lived an extraordinary life. Helping the poor, the Harijans, the helpless, afflicted women; raising her voice against domestic violence; picketing liquor shops; opposing and burning foreign goods; joining the prohibition movement; wearing khadi and inspiring others — with countless such things she was involved. Could any ordinary woman even imagine it? Only one in lakhs does this. She was president of the Mahila Mandal of Garhwal…

The Mahila Mandal (continued)

Its working office was right at our house in the upper bazaar of Pauri — a large tin box of registers and papers holding membership forms and minutes. Two other important members, to whom I still offer salutations — both calm, simple, virtuous, in their khadi dhotis — were Smt. Dobhal (whom we knew as Dobhalani Mausi), wife of the great freedom fighter and front-rank hill Congress leader Bhaskaranand ji Dobhal, renowned for his simplicity and honesty; and "Mantrani ji," Smt. Uniyal, who came from Srinagar (Garhwal) for the meetings. ("Mantrani" because the secretary was then called mantri.) Maa Ji and both women were also Congress workers. The Mahila Mandal's main work, as I know it, was:

  1. Protection of women's rights.
  2. Making women educated.
  3. Encouraging them to take up employment.
  4. Raising awareness about health.
  5. Education in making children, and daughters, educated.
  6. Taking part in social work alongside household duties.
  7. Awakening love for society and country.

Mother did all this work her whole life — in the Mahila Mandal, and after it.

A Living Example of Simple Living and High Thinking

When I now think of my mother's way of living and her diet, one memory comes from when we would feel a kind of anger at her plain dhoti and slippers — especially once we could bring her a nice khadi-silk sari ourselves; but she would not wear it, washing and wearing a dried-out dhoti of her own choosing instead. The funny thing is — the new sari we gave her, it later turned out, all her life she would give away to others she judged to need it more. Yet her dress sense was very clean, neat and graceful. Likewise her food: from the start she fed us food with very little salt and chili, never fried; she fed us every vegetable, dal, fruit — but when she herself ate, you'd never even know, and how much, we never knew…

Simple living (continued)

Later, when in the new house she was left first with Father and then, after his passing, all alone, the daily vegetables sometimes fell short; and if a guest arrived, she would pluck whatever raw or ripe vegetable grew in the garden, quickly fry it in an iron pan with a little cumin, coriander, turmeric, mustard oil and salt, bake rotis on the angithi, feed the guests, and feel delighted — and the food was very tasty too. Once or twice, coming home from Lucknow, we had the good fortune of eating it; that taste won't be found even at a five-star hotel. A woman whose children could give her every comfort, whose brother wanted to do everything for her — set apart from all this, she lived her whole life by her own ideals and values. There is a history of her social work too: in Dehradun, the village or Pauri, she was always extraordinary, fighting fearlessly against any oppression. Many men bore animosity towards her for standing against domestic cruelty; women found shelter at our place at night. On discrimination against Harijans and excesses in offices she sat on hunger strikes; on prohibition she went to jail 2–3 times. Without holding any office she fulfilled the human dharma. Our Mother was simple living steeped in high thinking.

She Never Let Shortcomings Stand in the Way of the Family's Future

Born into a large family, Father lived his whole life with a big outlook and authority, making constant efforts to give his children the best of everything. But with 8 children and a wealthy clan, keeping harmony was very difficult — and here Maa Ji's contribution was very great: to carry the whole enterprise forward she made her days and nights one, and by grace it moved successfully on.

Dedicated to Maa Durga, in Memory of Revered Father (poem)

Some breaths moved in the body, the feeling of public welfare you carried in your mind; walking, walking, when you grew tired, you found a path without end, O Father — standing firm on truth, you fought and stood, you went on struggling, always. Wearing the armour of silence, an idol of sacrifice, you walked on, the lesson you came to the field having learned — we walk on now within your blessing. O Mother Durga Devi, you recognized the truth, you joined the weak and the strong, how much you struggled, O Mother, to make life a sweet game.

Mistress of the Spirit of Selfless Service

I can say, from firm conviction and experience, that if Smt. Durga Devi Khanduri's services were measured against the posts — MLA, MP, minister, chief minister, governor, ambassador — that today's politicians attain through their social work, she should have been seated in some topmost post. But it was not so, for two reasons. First, she never took up service as a business for the sake of a post; second, she did not adopt the principle of "my position too, my pleasures too" — she did not serve and struggle in order to warm her hands at the comforts of life and at politics. Most of today's politicians change their post by their label, not their label by their post. Far from that, Smt. Durga Devi is even today just as she was — that same ordinary worker struggling for society, neither MLA nor MP.

Mantrani ji and Dobhalani Phuphu

Recalling those idols of simplicity, those true people of sacrifice and devotion to duty, there comes to mind Smt. Durga Devi's inseparable companion Mantrani ji, perhaps a mantri (secretary) in the Mahila Mandal or Zila Parishad. She, Smt. Durga Devi, and Dobhal Phuphu ji were perhaps the first real Congress workers and social workers of the hills.

As a True and Active Worker of the Mahila Mandal

Most people come into the world and just pass on; but the niyati (destiny) spoken of in Hindu philosophy is truly important in life. Smt. Durga Devi did, through that destiny, work very few manage. When Pauri was full of "babu culture," it was the influence of her faith and humanity that gave concrete shape to public-welfare programmes. From the early 1950s and for years she would finish Mahila Mandal work around midnight, after all the housework — filling registers, seeing to forms, planning, writing applications, sometimes with her children's help. And busy as she was, if a woman came with a problem she would go with her to the DM or another officer to try to solve it, then come home and return to her stove.

A Curious Blend of Tradition and Progress

If there is progress in one's thinking, and that progress is tied to public welfare, that alone I call progressiveness. I will give an example of Smt. Durga Devi's mental progress. She often said — "In begging there is begging, and winning the three worlds…"

Tradition and progress (continued)

She would explain: "Whatever you call your own is not really your own; it is given by someone, you are only the medium; so with whatever you have, distribute the surplus beyond your need among those who need it." And she did exactly this — besides giving the poor food, money and clothes, she took time from her own busy days to resolve their problems. This progress went on continuously, and she stayed engaged her whole life in service of greater scope and greater numbers.

Far From a Rich Clan's Daughter-in-Law: The Wish to Settle in the Village

In the history of feudal houses one sometimes sees a stranger arrive who does not get involved in the cunning and scheming, but keeps to the path of "walk apart, alone" — without arrogance, bound within limits, joined to tradition, struggling without a sound. Smt. Durga Devi showed this even as the daughter-in-law of a wealthy clan. In money, business and property the Khanduri clan was matched by few — 3 mansions in Mussoorie, 4 in Dehradun, 8 houses in Uttarkashi, a big house in Tehri, a whole neighbourhood in Srinagar, property in Rudrapur, vast village land, servants and managers — and though the brothers were charitable and kind, the weaknesses of big houses were there too. Unimpressed, taking only the good, keeping God in view on unbreakable principles, she fulfilled her human duty, and in 1942 left the splendour of Dehradun for the hills and the campaign of struggle.

An Inborn Tendency to Struggle Against Adversity

Smt. Durga Devi says — from childhood she was considered the weakest and simplest of the sisters; no one took her seriously; she was behind in eating and in demanding new clothes, but ahead in looking after the younger siblings and the housework. In her heart she understood that adding to the problems of so large a family would only grow the parents' suffering — so she bound her heart down. (This habit of "binding the heart" set so deep that her life passed only in hardships — for in-laws, for siblings, for husband, for children.) That very process of self-denial later became a help in giving up things for the downtrodden, the poor, the helpless and the afflicted.

Smt. Durga Devi — Unforgettable Works

  1. Founding the Mahila Mandal in Pauri district.
  2. Picketing for prohibition.
  3. As a jail visitor, resolving prisoners' problems.
  4. Gherao of the CMO against the harassment of women.
  5. Organizing regional development seminars.
  6. Promoting Hindu–Muslim harmony.
  7. Developing women's awareness of education, employment and rights.
  8. Getting a school opened in every village.
  9. Awakening enthusiasm for bhajan, kirtan and social programmes.

Bahuguna ji and Didi Durga

It is said that family traits leave their mark somewhere. In the personalities of Bahuguna ji and Didi Smt. Durga Devi I often see much in common — though their direction, aim, scale and priorities differed. The courage of conviction, lofty thinking, helping the weak, capacity for struggle, a vast heart, sincerity, fearlessness, compassion, the urge to help, supreme courage, doing good to others — these common traits were such that one could say their thinking was one, the boundaries different. Fortunately these similarities often expressed themselves as harmony, begun in Dehradun when Bahuguna ji came to his sister's home to study, and persisting through politics. Whatever his programme — in the hills, Lucknow, or Bombay — she was eager to take part; and he was eager to see her at the very biggest rally. When he addressed a big rally at Begum Hazrat Mahal with the late Smt. Indira Gandhi on the stage, he said: "In this crowd my sister Durga Devi Khanduri too has come from the hills. In my struggle she has been a great support — she got me my first bicycle from Darshan Lal Gate, and is the inspiration to struggle." All the more so because both were not ordinary people but personalities of vast heart and mind.

The True Form of Humanity: Durga Devi Khanduri

The traditional fighting of religions, and destruction in religion's name, has today grown more violent; and with it run the conflicts of caste, colour and region — enmity, bitterness, utter destruction. It is perhaps destiny that this has gone on since primordial times; the form may differ, but ugliness is one and the same. Amid this evil, some personalities arrive who, like stars, kindle the lamp of hope and save society from total darkness. Such a one I speak of — Smt. Durga Devi Khanduri. How much complexity she carried, yet how simple; after how many struggles, still calm; however helpless, still helpful; how broken, yet pressing toward the future; rising from how great a void, passing through the crooked winding trails of life, fighting her way to the far shore and never forgetting it. Service to society, selflessness, the true form of humanity — that is Durga Devi.

Doing Good to Others is the Supreme Dharma

Apart from her, I have never seen a householder so sacrificing and so dedicated to others' welfare. Even surrounded by personal hardships, she was always ready with time and help for others. In the beginning in Pauri she passed through great struggle, but from morning to evening there was a stream of the poor — women and men — and even with limited means she gave them solid help.

She is Not Our Mother, She is the Mother of the World by the late Dr. Naveen Chand Khanduri

In our Niti-Sangraha it is written: "In this changing world, who is there who does not die? But his birth alone is blessed, by whom his lineage is uplifted."

When I went to Prayagraj for the last rites of my ever-memorable, supremely revered "Mama" — the embodiment of boundless love, the late Shri Hemvati Nandan ji Bahuguna — I learnt for the first time that he was beloved of the whole world. Until then I thought perhaps he loved only me so much. But on that sorrowful day the illusion was dispelled. Wherever I went in that Kumbh-like crowd, all had the same one grief: "Alas, today my father has departed — the protector of all." So just as Bahuguna ji was father of the world, his elder sister (whom he regarded as a mother) is worshipped as the mother of the world. So much so that "Mama" always sat beside her as a small child sits beside its mother in longing for affection.

"Mother" was born in village Budhani of Garhwal district, in the home of the late Shri Revati Nandan ji Bahuguna, then the village patwari. From childhood she was brave, intelligent, firm of resolve and clever; her father had sensed this, and so she was given the name Durga Devi — a name as valorous and all-powerful as Durga. They say the great Shri Mahanand Dangwal of Kirtinagar, who knew astrology, looked at her birth-chart and was astonished — a unique, unprecedented, brilliant horoscope. He told our grandfather (the late Shri Radhavallabh ji Khanduri) that this girl would be most suitable for our Father. The match was settled and the marriage fixed.

The Mother of the World (continued)

"Mama" often told the story of the wedding. After the engagement, our Nanaji (an ordinary patwari) sent word to Pandit Radhavallabh ji — the all-in-all of our family, associated with great "Maldari" (wealth) — to please bring as few people as possible in the baraat. In reply Pandit ji wrote that, if so, he would send only "Varnarayan" and a pandit with him — a sarcastic message. When "Nana" got it, sitting in the courtyard at Budhani puffing his hookah, he flared up, paced about, and had a letter written: "Maldar Sahab, you are big people, but that does not mean you can insult us. Bring however many you wish — but on one condition: not a single member of the party will come on foot; every one will come mounted on a horse." On the wedding day, "Mama" said, one member of the party was in Budhani while the last was still in Srinagar-Garhwal — so long was the baraat; tents had to be pitched across the village. "Nana" later said he was paying off the debt of that wedding for years. Such a wedding had never taken place in Garhwal, nor would there be again — Durga herself in her vast cosmic form.

After the marriage "Mother" stayed in our village Margadana, where I was born. A few months later they went to Dehradun, where Father was a director in an insurance company and ran his own business, and built a magnificent mansion at 12-New Road, Dalanwala (today Hotel Aroma). It was here that "Mama" came from Garhwal and was admitted to D.A.V. for class ten. His entry into politics is linked to our Mother: one day he brought from the Dehradun library a book by Dr. Pattabhi Sitaramayya on the freedom struggle, and told Mother of the British atrocities on Indians, at which her tears flowed. At that moment, he said, he resolved to one day uproot British rule — and so his political life began. He always credited his "Didi" for his political successes. We returned to Pauri-Garhwal around 1942–43 because "Mother" wanted to live near her ancestral village. Father's finances were poor and raising eight children was no easy task, but by Mother's intelligence, courage, patience and skill our cart rolled forward; all we are today is because of her. When she recently fell ill, people in the hospital said to her, "If something happens to you, what will become of us?" — such was the trust she carried. (Signed) Dr. Naveen Chandra Khanduri, Deputy Secretary, Mines (retired). (These remembrances were written before Maa Ji's passing.)

My Mother-in-Law, Rich in Courage and Daring by Harshalata Khanduri

The supreme courage seen in Smt. Durga Devi will not be found even among far more educated people. Though not very educated, she was wise; in service, very generous; and though her body was not strong, she had amazing daring — the habit of letting no shortfall stop whatever she set her mind to. Day and night her mind pressed toward the future and toward public welfare, heedless of the struggle, ready to do anything for humanity. To such a personality, salutations and reverence.

Maa Ji and the Congress by Mohan Khanduri

Even today the Congress takes concrete form in my mind in two ways — when I see the tricolour, and when I remember my mother wrapped in a plain khadi dhoti. How coarse the khadi, yet how great the path. If that quality were to enter today's Congress members or politicians, India would become the golden bird once more. Running the charkha was a rule in our house. Villagers came with their problems, and she, hearing them, would finish her work and set off wherever the work might get done; she even cut her own expenses to help the poor and do the Congress party's work.

An Interview with Grandmother (Dadi ji) taken before the author's marriage — a glimpse of her thrilling life

This is from April 1986. Papa and Mummy had brought us to Pauri for the holidays. I, Papa, Atul, Pallavi and Micky were small; we sat in Dadi's hut-like room. Papa had long wanted to write about Dadi, and suddenly said — "Take down an interview of Dadi ji, showing the various aspects of her struggle-filled life." I asked Dadi, and right there in her room, paper and pen from her own attaché, on 24.06.86, I asked her these questions:

Q: What name were you called by in childhood?
A: Anandi.

Village Budhani. Mother's name, Punni Devi. Because no brother or sister came after 6 girls, Father had 14 children from the two mothers of a second marriage. In childhood she would not eat unless Mother gave it.

Birth of brother Bahuguna ji — When I was seven the brother was born; there was great celebration.

Father — Very well-off; grandfather and Father were patwaris in the English era; Father later a kanungo; servants, horses, and authority over the region.

Education — Up to class 4 at Devalgarh, a lot for girls then.

Childhood friend — Janaki, the only daughter of the village Seth ji.

House — A grand mansion, the only one in the region, with cut-stone walls, courtyard and balconies.

Engagement — At 13½, when Father was at Guptkashi, word came that the in-laws would bring ornaments. There had been many proposals, but Father accepted only this one. On the engagement day she had worn her sari inside-out, and her aunt set it right.

Wedding — The first of its kind in the whole district. Father wished a simple wedding, but the wealthy in-laws said 400 would come in the party, on horses. The late Radhavallabh ji, who attended no one's wedding, came; representatives of Maharaj Tehri too. The drummer alone got 25 gold guineas. It was the wedding of the eldest son of the wealthy family; she wept much at the farewell.

The interview (continued)

Husband and in-laws — She saw him first 6 months after the wedding — generous-hearted, learned and good. Among 6 brothers- and 6 sisters-in-law she was the eldest daughter-in-law. Sons — 7 years after the wedding the first son, Naveen Chandra, was born in the village; 3 months later they went to Dehradun. Family — 4 sons and 4 daughters who serve well. Husband — struggled greatly yet walked only on principle; a very great journalist (in English). In-laws' business — timber merchants, trading to Punjab and Calcutta. Lifestyle — 4 mansions in Dehradun, two in Mussoorie; summers in Mussoorie, winters in Dehradun. Children Bhuvan Chandra, Pratibha, Pushpa and Pramila were born in Dehradun; Vijaya, Mohan and Sudhir in Pauri.

Atmosphere — Nehru ji, Malaviya ji and others visited. Father-in-law was awarded the title Rai Bahadur, which he returned. Work for society and freedom — first, starting to wear khadi, voting and campaigning for Mahavir Singh of Congress; then coming to Pauri. In 1942, during the great war, she came with brother Hemvati from Allahabad. In Pauri: household work with the Mahila Mandal, Congress work, prabhat-pheris, helping the poor, promoting self-respect and education among women.

Village — "I always wish our village stay green, and my children come and serve. I have got a school and a dispensary opened here, given free land, and will give more for good work." Husband — "He passed in 1972; since then I am alone; the children come; my brother is my only support." Last wish — "To spend my remaining time in the service of Lord Krishna and with my sons." So this was a small glimpse of our great, struggling, learned, industrious Dadi ji — going on selflessly, without any self-interest.

Restoration of the Village's Old House and the Family Goddess

An astonishing thing: when I was in Pauri I often went to the village and worshipped Maa Dyurati Devi. After I left Pauri — especially after a job, marriage and children — I went almost every year with my family for darshan. Sitting to worship in my Lucknow home, the wish to repair the village temple and house grew strong. About 25–30 years ago I made a large tin box and resolved to keep putting money into it as I could, to use for this work. For years the box filled — and one day Bhuvan brother telephoned that the village house was to be rebuilt and the temple repaired, and all the brothers' cooperation was needed. I said I would do whatever I could, for this was the very wish of my heart. Bhuvan brother did the main work and we contributed; the box was very helpful. Today a fine temple of Mother sits in the village with the other deities. Om Jai Mata Di. (Here too, Om Narsingh Narayan deities dwell on the hill.)

That the Affection Among Brothers and Sisters Has Remained is a Matter of Good Fortune

We siblings — Naveen, Bhuvan, Pratibha didi, Pushpa didi, Vijaya, Sudhir and I — even if we are not so connected in a practical sense today, emotionally I can say with conviction that we remain just as our mother and father raised us. Here, briefly, their roles:

Father — very simple, guileless, truthful — simple within, but regular and disciplined outwardly. Renouncing a vast inheritance, name, wealth and property that lakhs never get, he chose the secluded, happy life of Pauri over Dehradun-Mussoorie, and lived on his own hard work and ability, without desiring more, keeping from cunning, succeeding in giving all of us abundant education and life, and remaining self-reliant to his last moment. (That self-reliance I too have tried to live.) To his last breath he became dear to God without becoming a burden — an ideal father, from whom every sibling received something priceless.

Mother — rich in many dimensions, simple yet firm; though not fully educated herself, able to educate the educated; she made it her whole life's offering to teach restraint and discipline to a large family, and the seed she sowed of "becoming something in life" she herself manured and watered, sacrificing her whole life to make it a fruit-bearing tree — taking in return neither fruit nor grain, only thinking, all her life striving so that love would remain among us siblings.

An example — When she came to me in Lucknow (where Bahuguna Mamaji also lived), I have the satisfaction of arranging her journey to Rameshwaram twice, as she wished, seeing her there and back through my colleague Tiwari ji. She would say with joy, "Daughter-in-law, when I am at your place, it feels as if I've come to my own maternal home." When she came she would speak of the other siblings' problems, so that any indifference would be understood by its cause; and going to the others' homes she would speak of mine — so that no ill-feeling grew between us, and, importantly, she would resolve some problem at whichever house she visited. It is by her grace that we stand where we are today. Glory to God and the gods and goddesses.

End of the booklet.
